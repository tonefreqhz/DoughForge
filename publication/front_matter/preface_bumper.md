# Preface to the Bumper Edition

Kipling gave us six honest working men — *What* and *Why* and *When*
and *How* and *Where* and *Who* — and for most of my working life I
have tried to keep them all employed simultaneously, which is, I will
admit, an unreasonable demand to make of any workforce, honest or
otherwise. The Carroll alternative is no more reassuring: in *The
Hunting of the Snark*, the Bellman's crew would believe a thing to be
true if it were repeated thrice. We have not, as a civilisation,
improved much on this method. The thrice-repeated assertion remains the
primary unit of public epistemology. The honest working men are largely
on short time.

These were not abstract problems when I came to write the FAIR-Index
paper. They were operational ones. The workflow was Python-heavy — the
graphing required it — and the dataset had first to be made less
*spread-sheety*, which is to say: less legible to human intuition and
more legible to machine process. This is not a trivial translation. A
spreadsheet is a kind of argument. It has a shape, a direction, an
implied reader who will follow the columns with their eye and feel the
weight of the accumulation. Strip that structure out, flatten it into
something a parser can traverse, and you have gained machine
readability at the cost of something harder to name — the *sense* of
the data, the way it breathes.

I had spent over four years meticulously updating that spreadsheet,
watching the figures unfold as all functions and algorithms do in their
peculiar heartless poetry. Which brings me to a point I cannot leave
unmade:

---

*Metaphors for Tyranny*

A poem is like an
algorithm. Not
in itself but similar.
Algorithms are similes for
poetry.

Algorithms are not like
poems which are
streams of consciousness that
enliven and awaken.

A metaphor that inspires the
imagination of possibilities.
Not a modelled conformity of
deterministic tyranny.
Algorithms are a metaphor
for tyranny.

*Roger G Lewis, 2025*

---

The question the FAIR-Index work kept returning me to was not
methodological but epistemological — the Foucauldian one, if you will
permit the name-drop (and you should, because he earned it): *who gets
to decide what counts as data, and what counts as reading it correctly?*
What are the good of conventional signs if the data they produce is
read out of context, partially consumed, and the rest assumed? The
partial consumption is not accidental. It is, in many cases, the
point. The Noble Lie does not require fabrication. It requires
selective attention, and selective attention is easy to arrange when
the people doing the arranging also design the instruments of
measurement.

This collection is the result of fifteen years of research across
music, digital signal processing, philosophy, and the considerably
less glamorous curriculum of living — father, husband, son, brother,
friend, employer, exile, and recovering multi-millionaire (the
recovery, I should note, has been more instructive than the
accumulation). The Bumper Edition extends the ePub with additional
material that did not fit the original scope, not because it was less
important but because scope is itself a political act, and I was not
ready, the first time, to make all of the cuts the argument required.

I am ready now. Or near enough.

As my grandfather would say — and as Foster has already said, with
rather more authority than I can muster — I should keep my sentences
shorter and weigh anchor on my reminiscences.

So. Here is the work. Read it as a report from underground. Measure it
against what you already know to be true.

That is all I ask.

*Roger G Lewis*
