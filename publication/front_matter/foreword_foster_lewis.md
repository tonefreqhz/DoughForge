# FOREWORD

*By Foster Lewis, author of* The Miner's Tale

---

I will tell you something about the way coal sits in the hand.

It is heavier than you expect, the first time. Not because it is dense,
though it is dense, but because you know what it cost to bring it up.
Every lump that came out of the Rhondda had a man behind it — his back,
his breath, his years. We did not speak of this in those terms. We did
not need to. The weight said it plainly enough to anyone willing to
hold it.

My grandson Roger has spent a considerable portion of his life trying
to explain to people what has replaced the coal. He writes about money
that exists only as numbers on screens, about wealth created without
labour, about systems so abstract that the men who operate them cannot
always say what it is they are doing or who, in the end, pays for it.
I have read his work with the attention of a man who learned to read
late and has never since taken the privilege lightly. I do not always
follow the technical argument. But I follow the direction of it, and
the direction is always the same: *behind every abstraction is a human
being, and someone is paying, and it is not the person who designed
the system.*

That is not a new observation. We knew it in the pit. We knew it in
the 1921 strike, when the owners locked us out and the government stood
behind the owners and the newspapers explained that this was the natural
order of things. We knew it when the means test inspector came to the
door and counted the furniture before he would allow relief. The system
has always had a human cost that the system prefers not to itemise.
My grandson itemises it. That is his work, and it is necessary work,
and I am glad someone is doing it, even if I sometimes wish he would
use shorter sentences.

The tradition he belongs to is older than he knows, or perhaps older
than he admits. The miners of South Wales were readers. We had our
libraries — the Workmen's Institute at Tonyrefail, the reading rooms,
the debating societies. We read Burns and Tennyson and Shakespeare not
because we were told to but because the words named things we had
already experienced and could not otherwise have put into language.
When Omar Khayyam wrote of turning down an empty glass, we understood
him. We had turned down a few ourselves.

Roger's work extends that tradition into territory we could not have
imagined — into the mechanisms of central banking, the philosophy of
consciousness, the poetry of electromagnetic fields. I will not pretend
to have followed him everywhere he has gone. But I recognise the
impulse. It is the same impulse that sent men down into darkness with
a lamp and a canary: the need to know what is actually there,
regardless of what the management has told you is there.

The canary, for those who do not know, was the early warning. A small
bird in a small cage, carried into the workings where the air was
uncertain. When the canary stopped singing, you did not debate the
matter. You moved. The bird's silence was not an opinion. It was a
fact, and you acted on it.

I have wondered, in my later years, what serves as the canary now.
The air of public life has seemed to me, for some time, to carry
something that does not smell right. The poets, I notice, have been
among the first to say so. They are usually the first. When language
begins to be used to obscure rather than to illuminate, when words are
made to mean their opposites, when the official account and the lived
experience diverge so completely that people stop trusting either —
the poets notice. They may not be able to fix it. But they name it,
and the naming matters.

This collection is an act of naming. It is the work of a man who has
gone down into the data as we went down into the coal, and who has
brought up what he found, and who has shared it with anyone willing
to read it. Whether it is coal or fool's gold, I leave to you to judge.
I was never much for telling people what to think. In the mines, you
learned quickly enough that a man who cannot read the seam for himself
is a danger to everyone around him.

Read this work for yourself, then. Read it with the attention you would
give to any report from underground — not with deference, and not with
dismissal, but with the honest willingness to consider what is being
said and to measure it against what you already know to be true.

That is all any writer can ask. It is all I ever asked.

*Foster Lewis*
*Tonyrefail, Rhondda Valley*
*Written in his eighty-third year, with the patient assistance of his wife Doris*
